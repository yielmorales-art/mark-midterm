Chapter 1 — Introduction to Marketing

Core Concepts ▾

What is Marketing?

Marketing is the process of creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large. It is not just advertising — it is everything a company does to attract and keep customers.

Marketing Orientations

  • Production Concept: Consumers prefer products that are widely available and low-cost. Companies focus on production efficiency.
  • Selling Concept: Consumers won't buy enough unless the company aggressively promotes and sells its products.
  • Marketing Concept: Focus on understanding and satisfying customer needs better than competitors.
  • Societal Marketing Concept: Balance customer satisfaction, company profit, and long-term societal well-being.

Key Terms

A target market is the specific group of customers a company aims to reach. Customer value is the perceived benefit minus the perceived cost of a product in the buyer's mind. Marketing aims to create and deliver this value.

Chapter 2 — Marketing Strategy & Planning

SWOT · 4 Ps · Ansoff ▾

SWOT Analysis

A strategic planning framework that evaluates a company's Strengths and Weaknesses (internal factors) alongside Opportunities and Threats (external factors). It guides strategy by revealing where the company stands relative to its environment.

The Marketing Mix — 4 Ps

  • Product: What you sell — features, design, branding, packaging.
  • Price: What you charge — pricing strategy, discounts, perceived value.
  • Place: Where and how you distribute — channels, logistics, coverage.
  • Promotion: How you communicate — advertising, PR, sales promotions, digital marketing.

Ansoff Matrix — Growth Strategies

  • Market Penetration: Existing product, existing market — sell more of what you have.
  • Market Development: Existing product, new market — expand into new geographies or segments.
  • Product Development: New product, existing market — innovate for your current customers.
  • Diversification: New product, new market — highest risk, highest potential reward.

Chapter 3 — Market Environment

PESTLE · Micro · Macro ▾

The Marketing Environment

A company operates within two layers of environment: the microenvironment (factors close to the company) and the macroenvironment (broader societal forces). Understanding both is essential for strategic planning.

Microenvironment

Includes factors that directly affect a company's ability to serve customers:

  • Suppliers: Provide resources; price or availability changes directly affect production.
  • Competitors: Other companies in the same market.
  • Customers: The individuals or organizations buying the product.
  • Intermediaries: Distributors, retailers, and agents who help deliver products.

Macroenvironment — PESTLE

  • Political: Government policies, trade laws, political stability.
  • Economic: Inflation, interest rates, consumer purchasing power.
  • Social: Demographics, cultural trends, lifestyle changes.
  • Technological: Innovation, digitalization, automation.
  • Legal: Labor laws, consumer protection, intellectual property.
  • Environmental: Climate change, sustainability, green expectations.

Chapter 4 — Segmentation & Positioning

STP Framework ▾

The STP Framework

Marketing strategy revolves around three connected steps: Segmentation (dividing the market), Targeting (choosing which segments to serve), and Positioning (deciding how to be perceived).

Types of Market Segmentation

  • Demographic: Age, income, gender, occupation, education.
  • Geographic: Country, region, city, climate, urban vs. rural.
  • Psychographic: Lifestyle, values, personality, attitudes.
  • Behavioral: Purchase frequency, brand loyalty, usage rate, attitude toward product.

Targeting Strategies

  • Undifferentiated: One marketing mix for the whole market.
  • Differentiated: Separate strategies for multiple segments.
  • Concentrated (Niche): All focus on one small, specialized segment.

Positioning

Positioning is how a brand is perceived in the minds of target consumers relative to competitors. A positioning map plots brands on axes (e.g., price vs. quality) to visualize competitive space.

Chapter 5 — Understanding Consumer Behavior

Maslow · Decision Process ▾

Why Do Consumers Buy?

Consumer behavior is shaped by psychological (motivation, perception, learning), personal (age, occupation, lifestyle), and social (family, reference groups, culture) factors.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Abraham Maslow argued human needs exist in a pyramid — lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones become motivating:

  • 1. Physiological: Food, water, shelter, sleep.
  • 2. Safety: Security, stability, freedom from fear.
  • 3. Social (Belongingness): Love, friendship, belonging.
  • 4. Esteem: Recognition, respect, achievement.
  • 5. Self-Actualization: Fulfilling one's full potential and finding meaning.

Consumer Decision-Making Process

  • 1. Problem Recognition: Buyer realizes a need or problem.
  • 2. Information Search: Looks for solutions — ads, reviews, word-of-mouth.
  • 3. Evaluation of Alternatives: Compares competing products on key criteria.
  • 4. Purchase Decision: Chooses a product and buys.
  • 5. Post-Purchase Behavior: Reflects on whether the product met expectations; may experience satisfaction or buyer's remorse (cognitive dissonance).

Chapter 6 — B2B Buying Behavior

Buying Center · Derived Demand ▾

B2B vs. B2C

Business-to-business (B2B) markets differ from consumer (B2C) markets in important ways:

  • B2B purchases are typically larger in volume and more rational.
  • B2B buying involves multiple people in a formal decision process.
  • B2B demand is derived — it depends on end-consumer demand.
  • B2B relationships tend to be longer-term with contracts and negotiations.

The Buying Center

In B2B, purchase decisions involve a buying center — a group of people with different roles:

  • Initiator: Identifies the need for a purchase.
  • Influencer: Shapes specifications and recommendations.
  • Decider: Has final authority to approve the purchase.
  • Buyer: Handles the actual procurement transaction.
  • User: The person who ultimately uses the product.
  • Gatekeeper: Controls the flow of information to the group (e.g., an assistant screening vendor calls).

B2B Buy Situations

  • Straight Rebuy: Routine repurchase with no changes.
  • Modified Rebuy: Repurchase with some changes to specs or supplier.
  • New Task: Buying something for the first time — most complex.

Derived Demand

Demand for B2B products is derived from demand for consumer goods. If demand for cars drops, demand for steel also drops — even if the steel company did nothing wrong.

Chapter 7 — Marketing Research

Primary · Secondary · Qualitative ▾

What is Marketing Research?

Marketing research is the systematic process of gathering, analyzing, and interpreting information to help make better marketing decisions. It reduces the risk of costly mistakes.

Types of Data

  • Primary Data: Collected firsthand for the specific research problem at hand — surveys, interviews, focus groups, observations.
  • Secondary Data: Already-existing data collected by someone else for a different purpose — census reports, industry publications, government data.

Research Approaches

  • Qualitative Research: Explores motivations and attitudes through open-ended methods — focus groups, in-depth interviews, ethnography. Small sample sizes. Answers "why?"
  • Quantitative Research: Gathers numerical data from large samples — surveys with structured questionnaires, experiments. Answers "how many?" and "how much?"

The Research Process

  • 1. Define the problem and research objectives.
  • 2. Develop the research plan (methods, data sources).
  • 3. Collect the data.
  • 4. Analyze the data.
  • 5. Present findings and make recommendations.

Chapter 8 — Product Planning

Product Levels · Branding · Mix ▾

Three Levels of a Product

  • Core Product: The fundamental benefit or solution the customer is actually buying. (A drill buyer wants holes, not the drill.)
  • Actual Product: The physical product — features, design, brand name, packaging, quality level.
  • Augmented Product: Additional services and benefits — warranty, delivery, after-sales service, installation.

Product Line vs. Product Mix

  • Product Line: A group of related products under one brand that serve similar functions (e.g., all Head & Shoulders shampoo variants).
  • Product Mix: The total set of all product lines a company offers. Described by width (number of lines) and depth (variants per line).

Branding

A brand is a name, symbol, or design that identifies a seller's product and differentiates it from competitors. Strong brands build brand equity — the added value a brand name brings to a product.

"A product is something made in a factory; a brand is something that is bought by a customer." — Stephen King

Chapter 9 — Innovation & New Product Development

PLC · NPD Process · Cases ▾

What is Innovation?

Innovation means creating something new or improving something existing to create value. Companies innovate because consumer needs change, technology evolves, competition intensifies, and markets become saturated.

Three Levels of Innovation

  • Continuous Innovation: Small, incremental improvements. Doesn't change behavior. (e.g., New Coke Zero flavor, new chip flavor.)
  • Dynamically Continuous Innovation: Moderate change, new form factor. Requires some behavior adjustment. (e.g., Foldable phones replacing traditional smartphones.)
  • Discontinuous Innovation: Completely new product requiring entirely new consumer behavior. (e.g., Netflix replacing DVD rentals; smartphones replacing cameras, maps, music players.)

Product Life Cycle (PLC)

  • Introduction: Low sales, high cost, limited competition. Focus: build awareness. (e.g., Apple Vision Pro.)
  • Growth: Rising sales, increasing competition, cost falling. Focus: market acceptance and brand preference. (e.g., TikTok, EVs.)
  • Maturity: Peak and leveling sales, intense price competition. Focus: differentiate and defend. (e.g., Shampoo, smartphones.)
  • Decline: Falling sales, reduced competition. Options: improve, reduce costs, or drop. (e.g., DVD players, fax machines.)

Products can move from Decline back to Growth through rebranding, new designs, or celebrity partnerships — as seen with Crocs and Polaroid/Instax.

New Product Development (NPD) — 5 Steps

  • 1. Idea Generation: From customers, employees, competitors, market trends, technology.
  • 2. Screening: Filter by feasibility, cost, and market potential.
  • 3. Idea Evaluation: Concept testing — surveys and focus groups to validate demand.
  • 4. Development: Build a prototype — beta apps, test recipes, early models.
  • 5. Commercialization: Full launch — manufacturing, distribution, advertising, pricing.
Funnel reality: 100 ideas → 20 screened → 5 evaluated → 2 developed → 1 launched. Most ideas never reach the market — and that's by design.

Why Products Fail

80–90% of new products fail. Common reasons: no real customer need, poor timing, too expensive, weak marketing, or poor quality. The New Coke case (1985) shows that even positive blind taste tests can miss emotional and cultural attachment. Kodak invented the digital camera in 1975 but withheld it to protect film revenue — and eventually fell behind entirely.
